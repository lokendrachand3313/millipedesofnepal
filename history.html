History of Nepalese Millipede Description

The history of millipede taxonomy in Nepal is rich and extensive, beginning with Carl's 1935 description of Orthomorpha (Orthomorpha) simulans and Hingstonia eremita, the latter of which established the genus Hingstonia.

1936–1961: Attems introduced the new genus Kophosphaera based on Kophosphaera excavata (Butler, 1874). After a long gap, Demange (1961) described two species of Gonoplectus from Nepal, namely G. hyatti and G. brolemanni, collected by K. H. Hyatt in 1954.

1970s–1980s: Shear (1979) described a new subfamily, Nepalelinnae, with the new genus Nepalella. In 1983, Mauries described Trachyiulus wilsonae and Podoglyphiulus elegans nepalensis. In 1986, Golovatch reported the family Polydesmidae from Nepal for the first time, describing four species of Usbekodesmus and the new genus Himalodesmus.

1990s: Golovatch recorded Orthomorpha coarctata and Oxidus gracilis for the first time in Nepal. He introduced several new genera including Martensosoma, Nepalomorpha, and Parorthomorpha. In 1996, Shelley described the genus Nepalozonium.

2000–Present: Korsos described Anaulaciulus (2001) and Hirudicryptus quintumelementum (2009). Significant revision occurred in 2014 when Golovatch synonymized several genera into Delarthrum Attems, 1936. Most recently, Golovatch (2022-2023) described Epanerchodus telnovi, Chondromorpha greke, and Delarthrum telnovi.

(+1) = species unidentified to species level because of the absence of male specimens or geographical record.

A Brief History of Working on Millipedes

For my master's thesis, I aimed to explore a novel subject within the context of Nepal. Under the guidance of my professor and supervisor, I focused on the Diplopoda (millipedes). I conducted a comprehensive study of millipedes in urban forest patches within the Kathmandu Valley.

My research primarily involved the taxonomic description of millipedes, emphasizing the identification based on gonopod structures. The discovery of high levels of endemism in these millipedes sparked a deeper interest in the Diplopoda of Nepal, highlighting the unique biodiversity of the region.
